Subject: [leandevelopment] A different way to measure maturity

Clyde Cutting and Gary Jedynak are working on the concept of ‘assessment’ instead of certification to determine the level of skill a person has. (See http://groups.yahoo.com/group/AgileAssessment/).  Widely used in such fields as airplane pilots and even employment screening, assessments use inference to determine skill level.  The idea is that if you can demonstrate a certain capability, it can be inferred that you have a whole set of skills necessary to demonstrate that capability.  The trick, of course, is to find the right thing to assess to properly infer the necessary underlying skills.

 

When I go into an organization, I am often asked:  What measurements do you suggest we use for software development?  My answer is that the organization should measure the average time it takes to satisfy a customer need.  The measure of how fast an organization can deliver value has always been the key measurement used in lean organizations, whether we are talking manufacturing, logistics, or product development.  It works for software development too. 

 

I propose that the true measure of the maturity of a software development organization is the speed at which the organization can reliably and consistently deliver on a customer request.  (By the way, this time is directly proportional software-in-process; see the files section of http://groups.yahoo.com/group/softwareinprocess/)  Exactly when you start the clock and when you stop it will differ from one environment to another, and will change over time.  However, measuring the time it takes from perceiving a customer need to filling it is a better indicator of maturity than the CMM levels.  CMM levels measure detailed, individual process capability; measuring the end-to-end process capability gives a much better picture of overall organizational capability/maturity. 

 

In supply chain management, the term ‘cycle time’ is used to indicate the average time of an end-to-end process; for example, the average time it takes from receiving a customer order until it is filled.  In product development, ‘cycle time’ is used to refer to the average time it takes to develop a new product.  In software development, cycle time means measuring the time it takes from receiving a customer request until the software is deployed.  If a development organization has a short cycle time, one can ‘infer’ that all sorts of other capabilities are in place.  Only very well organized and disciplined organizations which are focused on delivering customer value and eliminating all non-value-adding activities can deliver value quickly. 

 

I therefore propose that development cycle time, or its equivalent, software-in-process, are the best measures of the maturity of a software development organization.
