> What they are saying is that the quality certificate itself has value
> independent of the value of conforming to a quality standard.

I was once part of an ISO 9001-certified software development company. The company went for the certification because one of its major customers was ISO-certified and needed all of the software it used in its certified process to come from certified vendors. The aim was not to improve quality (it didn't) but to get the certificate. We needed that certificate within some timeperiod or else... we would lose their business.

>  If you certify a standard and the certification becomes valuable,
> people will want the certificate and will want to achieve it as
> cost-effectively as possible.

After the successful audit, my manager claimed to everybody who would listen that he had "saved the day" by steering the auditors away from his development team. Says something about the value of the audit, if they didn't even see the developers. Says even more about the trust my manager had in his team :-)

> How then can we assess companies to ensure that no 'cheating' occurs?

We should not look at *what we do* but to *what the results are*. Can we reliably, consistently, quickly deliver what customers need?

We could (and did) cheat by learning to give standard answers to audit questions; by generating analysis and design documents which meant nothing and were never looked at, but could be produced when the auditors asked for them.... We went through the motions of executing our theoretical process and that fooled the auditors, because we had all the right answers and all the right paperwork.

What we couldn't fake: bug counts remained high; projects slipped continuously; projects got released when management got fed up with the interminable testing/bug fixing periods; code and design was awful; customer satisfaction decreased with each release... But the auditors were not interested in those measurements.

The ironic thing is: I'm sure that the easiest and cheapest way to get any kind of quality certification, is to actually improve your process and your product. That way you don't have to teach everybody to give the right answers, they can just give the real answers. And it makes everybody happier: customers, developers, management.

So: ASK NOT WHAT WE DO, LOOK AT THE RESULTS.
