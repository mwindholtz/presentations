Case Study: Recovery Team  (2002)

With 6 months to go in an 18-month schedule we calculated that the project would be 2 months late.  We based this on the current team velocity and on release planning estimates for the main C++ based XP team.

The 2-month slippage was communicated 3 months previously.  But now the recalculated finish date confirmed to management that something needed to be done.  So we determined that the last 4 stories in the release could be implemented separately while using a technology new for the company.  The new team was the recovery plan.  It was the Recovery Team.

 

The manager was very happy with the high productivity and team spirit of the main C++ team.

And we all agreed that the main C++ XP team should not be disturbed and a separate team should be started from scratch.  This would help us avoid the perils of Brooks law:  Adding people to a late project will make it later. We were adding a separate team to a late project.  This was possible because XP handles one feature (or Story) at a time.  The later stories remained independent enough to break off and assign to another team.

 

Yet there were a number of concerns.  The company had never deployed a Java server application and no programmers were currently available.  By the time programmers arrived we had a little more than 2 months to deliver the application.  They were new to the company, and new to XP, but they had experience with Java and IBM-VisualAge.

 

As coach I introduced the XP process to them.  We started writing tests, and began to work out our integration cycle.  The team actually started before PC’s were available so we worked on whatever equipment was available that day (or even half-day).   The Customer team was made up of two functional experts, a test coordinator, and a very knowledgeable government employee.   The functional experts and the government employee were each only available 2-days a week, while the test coordinator was primarily busy with the main C++ project.

 

It was originally assumed that the requirements were well understood.  The 4 stories were a re-implementation of an existing mainframe application.  The goal was to move it to a Unix server.  The team held an Exploration meeting that gave a basic scope to the project.  Next we moved into our iteration #1 planning game.  The goal was to estimate enough work for one first week.  The programmers were a little lost at this point because so much was unclear at the same time.  The team had never used XP: they had never programmed in test-first style.  They had never integrated every hour.  And they were quite suspicious of the value of Refactoring.  All of these are normal start-up reactions.   We were all conscious that the time frame was very short.  They worried that one simple step at a time would not get us to completion in 2 months.  I worried that they may not learn the XP style in time for the productivity boost to kick–in during this short timeframe.  The time it takes before the productivity boost begins is not related to programmer skill.  It is related to programmer acceptance.  Until the tests are being written with care and focus no programmer will believe that Refactoring can be so effective.

 

The original list of 4 stories tuned out in the end to be 9 stories.  The original 4 were broken into smaller Stories and additional Stories were added to handle issues of concurrency and data-clean-up that did not exist on the mainframe.  As so often happens requirements that appear well defined at the beginning need to be adjusted when the details of implementation are considered.  XP agility is a good answer to this phenomenon.  But while the list of stories grew, the velocity of the team did too.

 

The main project risk was that the company had not yet delivered a Java application into production.  A concurrent 40-person project named HGWF for Home-Grown Water-Fall (not it’s real name) had been trying to solve numerous Java production questions for the last 10 months.   The customer wanted to know how my small team could deliver in 2 months what HGWF could not do in 10!   Since this was the first concern, we made it the first story.  We built a “Hello World” program and moved it though all the steps needed to get it into production.  We moved the source code from VisualAge Repository to a Unix file system, then checked the code into the change tracking system, then prepared and tested the makefiles and additional scripts and also found a way to deliver the correct version of the java virtual machine.

 

We did this in the first two weeks of the project.  The trouble that HGWF was having I found out, was that they could not move code toward production because completed code was the last step in their Water Fall process.  It is a step they never did reach before being canceled.   The XP benefit here was that we focused on the problem with a small bit of code and answered one of biggest worries before any other code was written. 

 

Next the team worked on programming tasks.  The first story the team focused on was the simplest story.  The customer was interested in doing the most complex story first.  But they agreed that since the team was new, we should ease into the heavy lifting with a warm-up exercise.  The team was confident that they could build the easy story within the first week so that was the estimate.  And off we went to work!

 

It was quickly clear to the programmers that this simple task was going to involve more than they expected.  We needed to build a whole infrastructure and design to support talking to CORBA and the Oracle database.  It took two iterations to finish this first small and simple story.  The team was worried: a simple story had taken two weeks and we had only 6 to go.

 

On top of that, the database administrators estimated that it would take 2-3 weeks to implement any requested changes that we submitted.  Weeks for each change! Our entire schedule was only 8 weeks!   A minor panic wave swept over the team.  Ok, I wondered aloud, how do you do you evolve a design when the Database folks think in units of weeks?  We tried a few alternatives, including storing all our objects in files or database BLOBs so that the schema would never change.  The architecture standards experts rejected both alternatives.  Finally we decided to use the current schema’s from the mainframe system, translate them into RDBS format and submit that to the DB Administrators.   As we finished Stories we would send in additional requests and hope DB could respond.  There was a lot of discussion about spending extra time doing an up-front design so that we would “know” the schema.  But I kept asking: if we spend the extra time now are we guaranteed that we will not need a last minute change?  In fact no amount of design now could have predicted what we did eventually run into.  It was good that the team agreed to simply move on to the coding at this point.  As we coded we added patterns to insulate the core design from the eventual database changes.  

 

In order to continue coding while waiting for the database schema to become available, we used a number of strategies.  We used the TableModule concept from Martin Fowler’s upcoming book.  We tried using MockObjects but found that they were too cumbersome for our needs.  Lastly we settled on using an in-memory HyperSQL  database.

 

During the first few weeks the team earnestly tried to add tests as they went.  But this was usually after the production code was written.  It took between 3 and 5 iterations until test-first had really proven itself in the minds of the developers.  The value of test-first became clearer to the team as they struggled to integrate hourly.  Since we had two pairs working the pair closer to having running tests would be the first to integrate.  This made integrating easier for the pair that was keeping up with their testing.  The easiest way to keep up with testing – as it turns out – is to do test-first coding.  This is one of those neat feedback loops in XP where it is easier for programmers to do the correct thing.  And when they do the wrong thing they themselves suffer.

 

The next step came when they started Refactoring more often.   Refactoring is usually introduced only after the team has seen the value of testing.  So at about iteration 3, I made them aware of code duplication that needed to be removed.   We talked about how we could add a better design and then I paired with various team members to show the steps of Refactoring.  The first reaction of the programmers was annoyance that instead of adding new feature code, we were spending time in house keeping.  Soon they discovered that the tests were not sufficient to guard against problems appearing during refactoring.  Occasionally they discovered that Refactoring surfaced hidden and subtle bugs or code dependencies.  Since they already accepted the benefits of testing, they now came to see the benefits of rigorous testing.  During refactoring the size of the code base rapidly diminished.  At first they found this discouraging because they had worked so hard on writing that pile of code.  Next they were cheered by how easy it was becoming to add new features.

 

At this point the team was at about week 5.  They had accepted testing and refactoring.  They were adding missing tests and discovering refactoring opportunities on their own.  Best of all, the XP productivity boost had kicked-in.  During the next 3 weeks the team worked smoothly and with confidence.  Adjustments in requirements occurred frequently with minimal affect on the team moral or productivity.  

 

The Recovery Team played an important part in delivering the whole project on-time and on-budget.
